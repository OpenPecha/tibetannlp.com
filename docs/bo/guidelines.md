

- བཀྲ་ཤིས་ཀྱིས་དོན་གྲུབ་ལ་ཀུ་ཤུ་གཅིག་སྤྲོད་ཀྱི་འདུག་གས།
- ཨོའོ། སྤྲོད་ཀྱི་འདུག

In Tibetan, སྤྲོད་ཀྱི་འདུག has the complete meaning of the sentence above: "Tashi gives an apple to Dhondrup".

An English translation of this dialogue would be:
- Does Tashi give an apple to Dhondrup?
- Yes./Yes, he does.

"*Gives" is impossible.
If we want to impose reusing the verb from the question, we would have
"he gives it to him"
we can't have anything less than that.

"he does" shows that if something is optional, it is the argument, not the subject, because it is rendered by the pronoun "he".
In English and romance languages, you can't have a grammatical sentence without the subject slot filled.
That is why when there is no subject, a neutral pronoun is used, like in
"it rains"

The fact the subject syntagm must be present while the object syntagm can be omitted has to be rendered in the syntactic tree, and it is done by keeping at the highest level the subject syntagm while pushing the object syntagm inside the VP.

In Tibetan, on the other side, such a distinction does not exist as the answer སྤྲོད་ཀྱི་འདུག shows. The subject and the object can be omitted, and from the structural point of view, nothing differentiates them.
One consequence of this is that in Tibetan, one does not need to elaborate strategies to have a short answer. reusing the same sentence without the subject and object NPs gives an answer as short as its English counterpart. ཨོའོ། སྤྲོད་ཀྱི་འདུག and "Yes, he does" are both three syllables long.

Moreover, the role of a subject in English is to determine the conjugation of a verb. That is why an English sentence can only have one subject. In tibetan, one can have two or more subjects without any problem, such as in
ཤིང་པས་སྟ་རེས་ཤིང་བཅད།
This structure is possible in Tibetan because verbs don't depend on the subject(s) for their inflection.
Such a sentence must be turned into a different structure in English:
"The woodcutter splits a log with an axe"
You are forced to decide who/what is splitting the wood, the woodcutter or the axe, whereas it is possible to say both do in Tibetan.
So the behaviour of the subject is completely different in English and Tibetan.

For those reasons, from the point of view of Syntax, there is no subject as it is defined for English in Tibetan. Because of that, it is not justified anymore to treat differently the subject and object syntagms. Those two syntagms are simply one besides the other, on the same level as the VP because they can both be omitted.

Once we have accepted that the structure of a typical sentence is two syntagms followed by a VP, the questions that remains unanswered is how do we distinguish the subject from the object?
Two things allow to unambiguously distinguish the roles: the order of the syntagms and the presence of prepositional syntagms.
1. in case of two NP, the closer to the verb is the object and the farther is the subject. In case one is a PP, typically a ལ་དོན་ PP, it will be taken to be the object irrelevant of its location.
2. the syntagm that contains a བྱེད་སྒྲ་ post-position is unambiguously the subject, the other syntagm then becomes the object, even if it is the farther one.
